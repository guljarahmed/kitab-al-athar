87. ZAKĀT AL-FIṬR AND SLAVES

303. Muḥammad said, “Abū Ḥanīfah informed us saying, ‘Ḥammād narrated to us from Ibrāhīm concerning the ṣadaqah a man pays on behalf of every slave or free person, minor or adult, “It is a half of a ṣāʿ of wheat939 or a ṣāʿ of dates.”’”940

Muḥammad said, “We adhere to this, and if he pays a ṣāʿ of barley that will also suffice. Abū Ḥanīfah said, ‘Half a ṣāʿ of raisins941 also suffices for him,’ but our verdict is that only a ṣāʿ of raisins942 suffices.”

304. Muḥammad said, “Sufyān ath-Thawrī informed us from ʿUthmān ibn al-Aswad that al-Mujāhid said, ‘Everything other than wheat is ṣāʿ by ṣāʿ.’”

305. Muḥammad said, “Abū Ḥanīfah informed us from Ḥammād that Ibrāhīm said, ‘There is no zakāh on slaves and those who pay an impost,943 but if they are kept for trade there will be zakāh on their value.’”

Muḥammad said, “We adhere to this and it is the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfah, may Allah, exalted is He, have mercy on him.”

306. Muḥammad said, “Abū Ḥanīfah informed us from Ḥammād that Ibrāhīm said, ‘If slaves are kept for trade, then zakāh is paid on their value, on every two hundred dirhams, five dirhams.’”

Muḥammad said, “We adhere to this and it is the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfah, may Allah, exalted is He, have mercy on him.”

Footnotes

  1. Ash-Shāfiʿī said that in all of that one pays a ṣāʿ. Ibn al-Hamām mentioned from Mujāhid who said, “Every thing apart from wheat has a ṣāʿ and wheat has a half of a ṣāʿ.” Aṭ-Ṭaḥāwī narrated it from a large body of people. ↩
  2. Abū Yūsuf narrated the like of it on p.64. ↩
  3. Because it and wheat are close in meaning since all the parts of each of them are eaten entirely, whereas the date stone is discarded as is the bran of the barley. ↩
  4. Since raisins and dates are close together in purpose. ↩
  5. That which the slave pays to his master of a confirmed tax due from him. There is no disagreement that there is no zakāh to be paid on slaves unless they have been bought for trade. [Slaves can be allowed to go to work in the world on the condition that they pay a regular sum to their owners. Interestingly, this impost is called ḍarībah, the modern Arabic term for a tax.] – Trans. ↩
