49. THE PRAYER OF SOMEONE WHO FAINTS

169. Muḥammad said, “Abū Ḥanīfah informed us from Ḥammād that he asked Ibrāhīm about a sick man who fainted and thus missed the prayer, and he said, ‘If it is only for a single day, I prefer539 that he make up the prayers, but if it is more than that, he is excused540 inshā’Allāh, exalted is He.’”

Muḥammad said, “If he faints for a day and a night, he makes up the prayers,541 but if it is for more than that he does not have to make them up, and that is the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfah.”542

170. Muḥammad said, “Abū Ḥanīfah informed us from Ḥammād from Ibrāhīm that Ibn ʿUmar said about someone who is in a faint for a day and a night, ‘He should make up his prayers.’”

Muḥammad said, “We adhere to this, until he is unconscious for more than that, and that is the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfah.”

Footnotes

  1. The pronoun refers to the single day, but it would be more obvious to have a feminine pronoun [i.e., the prayers] and what is meant by a single day is to make up five prayers. ↩
  2. Because if it went beyond a single day, then the difficulty in making up the prayers is obvious. ↩
  3. This is from legal preference (istiḥsān) and the analogical deduction gives that he would not make up the prayer if the fainting is total for the time of a complete prayer because his incapacity was certain. The reason for the legal preference is that if the time becomes extended then the number of prayers missed will be too numerous and be troublesome to make up, but that if the time is short, there is no trouble. Numerous prayers is in the case where it is for more than a day and a night because that is comprised in the definition of repetition. ↩
  4. And of Abū Yūsuf. According to Mālik and ash-Shāfiʿī he is absolved of the need to make up prayers whether few or many, and according to the Ḥanbalis he must make up the prayers whether they are few or many. ↩
